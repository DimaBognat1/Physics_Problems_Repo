# Problem 9 — Change of reference frame (Copernican → geocentric model)

Earth and Mars move in circles around the Sun in the same direction.

The heliocentric model is given by the position vectors:

\[
\vec r_Z(t) = R_Z(\cos(\omega_Z t), \sin(\omega_Z t))
\]

\[
\vec r_M(t) = R_M(\cos(\omega_M t), \sin(\omega_M t))
\]

---

## 1. Motion in the heliocentric reference frame

In the heliocentric system the Sun is at the origin.

- Earth moves on a circle of radius \(R_Z\)
- Mars moves on a circle of radius \(R_M\)

Both planets move with angular velocities \(\omega_Z\) and \(\omega_M\).

The trajectories are circular.

---

## 2. Position of Mars relative to Earth

The position of Mars relative to Earth is:

\[
\vec r_{M/Z}(t) = \vec r_M(t) - \vec r_Z(t)
\]

Substitute the vectors:

\[
\vec r_{M/Z}(t) =
(R_M\cos(\omega_M t) - R_Z\cos(\omega_Z t),
R_M\sin(\omega_M t) - R_Z\sin(\omega_Z t))
\]

---

## 3. Components

\[
x_{M/Z}(t) = R_M\cos(\omega_M t) - R_Z\cos(\omega_Z t)
\]

\[
y_{M/Z}(t) = R_M\sin(\omega_M t) - R_Z\sin(\omega_Z t)
\]

These equations describe the apparent motion of Mars as observed from Earth.

---

## 4. Interpretation

In the heliocentric system both planets move in simple circular orbits.

However, when observed from Earth (geocentric frame), the trajectory of Mars becomes more complex and may include retrograde motion.

This explains the apparent backward motion of Mars observed in the sky.
